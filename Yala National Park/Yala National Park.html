Yala National Park

The park is best known for its variety of wild animals. It is important for the conservation of Sri Lankan elephants, Sri Lankan leopards and aquatic birds. ... It is one of the 70 Important Bird Areas (IBAs) in Sri Lanka. Yala harbours 215 bird species including six endemic species of Sri Lanka.

 Cultural importance 
      
Yala had previously served as a crossroads for various civilizations. The mythological Hindu antihero King Ravana is said to have founded his kingdom here, with Ravana Kotte, which is now bu ried in the sea, as its border. Because Yala lies on their trading route, seafaring sailors carried In do Aryan civilisation with them. A huge number of ancient, but badly damaged, tanks bear witness to a thriving hydraulic and agricultural civilisation that dates back to the 5th century BC. The par k area includes Situlpahuwa, which housed 12,000 arahants, as well as Magul Vihara, which was erected in 87 BC, and Akasa Chaitiya, which was built in the 2nd century BC.
 Physical features 
      
The Yala area is largely made up of Precambrian metamorphic rock that is divided into two serie s: Vijayan series and Highland series. Among the six soil types, reddish brown soil and low humi c grey soil are the most common. Yala is located in Sri Lanka's lowest peneplain, which stretches from Trincomalee to Hambantota. The area has a flat and gently undulating plain that runs to t he ocean, with an elevation of 30 metres (98 feet) close to the coast and rising to 100 – 125 metres ( 328 – 410 feet ) in the interior. The national park is located in a dry semi arid climate, with rain falling primarily during the northeast monsoon.
